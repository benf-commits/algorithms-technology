The System

How it actually works

The laws, the policies, the consent forms, and the process. Everything that governs how our children's photos end up on public Facebook pages. None of it was designed for AI.

The legal framework

Multiple layers of Australian law and policy govern how schools collect and publish children's images. Understanding these layers matters, because the gaps between them are where the problem lives.

Federal: The Privacy Act 1988

Under the Privacy Act, a photograph of an identifiable person is personal information. If the image also reveals racial or ethnic origin, religious beliefs, or other sensitive characteristics, it's sensitive information with even stricter handling requirements.

The OAIC (Office of the Australian Information Commissioner) guidance is clear: organisations should use "particular care in the handling the images and other personal information of children" and should seek "express consent after telling them, in as much detail as possible, about what their picture will be used for and who will be able to see it."

There's an important detail here. The federal Privacy Act applies to private schools, but NSW government schools are not directly covered by it. Instead, they fall under NSW state privacy law.

Sources
  • Posting photos and videos, OAIC
  • Children and young people, OAIC

NSW: The PPIP Act 1998

NSW government schools are covered by the Privacy and Personal Information Protection Act 1998 (PPIP Act). This is the state equivalent of the federal Privacy Act, and it applies to all NSW public sector agencies, including schools.

Under the PPIP Act, photographs and recordings of identifiable people are personal information. The Act sets out Information Protection Principles covering collection, use, accuracy, security, and disclosure of personal information.

Posting a child's photo on a public Facebook Page is a disclosure of personal information to the entire internet. The PPIP Act requires that disclosure be lawful, for a purpose the information was collected for, and with appropriate consent.

Sources
  • PPIP Act, Information and Privacy Commission NSW
  • Photographs and recordings, NSW Department of Education

Federal: The Online Safety Act 2021

The Online Safety Act gives the eSafety Commissioner authority to protect Australians from online harms. It covers cyberbullying, image-based abuse (sharing intimate images without consent), and illegal content.

The Act's Basic Online Safety Expectations require online platforms to ensure "the best interests of the child is a primary consideration in the design and operation of services likely to be used by children."

The Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act 2024 added requirements for platforms to prevent under-16s from having accounts, effective December 2025.

Sources
  • Learn about the Online Safety Act, eSafety Commissioner
  • Social media age restrictions, eSafety Commissioner

Coming: Children's Online Privacy Code

The OAIC is developing a Children's Online Privacy Code, mandated by the Privacy and Other Legislation Amendment Act 2024. The Code must be in place by 10 December 2026.

Key elements of the draft Code include:

  • Children's best interests as a primary consideration
  • Consent requirements before using children's personal information
  • The right for children to request deletion of their personal information
  • Coverage beyond just social media, including educational tools and apps

Public consultation on the exposure draft ran from March to June 2025. Parents raised specific concerns about school-related apps and platforms that store children's photos linked to their names, ages, and other identifying details.

Sources
  • Children's Online Privacy Code, OAIC
  • Public Consultation Report, OAIC

The NSW Department of Education policy

The NSW DoE has a specific Social Media Policy (PD-2011-0418) with detailed procedures for how schools use social media. This is the policy that directly governs how our children's photos end up on Facebook.

Schools are required to keep accounts public

This is a critical point. The NSW DoE procedures state that school social media accounts "must not restrict access or be set as 'private' or 'closed.'" The stated rationale is that "the main purpose for using a public platform is to reach a broader audience and build a stronger community, which includes extended family and friends of students and people in the local area."

The policy requires public posting

This means schools are not just choosing to post publicly. They are required by Department policy to keep their social media accounts open to the public. A school that wanted to protect its students by switching to a private Group would currently be acting against DoE procedures. This policy was written before Meta admitted to scraping public posts for AI training, before Clearview AI was found to have breached the Privacy Act, and before deepfakes of Australian schoolgirls made national news. It needs to be updated.

Who runs the accounts

  • Schools must submit an EDConnect Online Social Media Request before creating accounts
  • The principal is responsible for overseeing and approving all account activities
  • A minimum of 2 administrators is required for each account
  • At least one administrator must be a principal or other school executive
  • Accounts are monitored during school hours only (9am-3pm weekdays, excluding holidays)

What can be posted

Student images can only be posted if the school holds a signed Permission to Publish form. The consent form must identify the specific social media platforms and explain what types of information will be used.

What cannot be posted:

  • Student information without documented parental consent
  • Confidential department information
  • Content that defames or harms reputation
  • Endorsed products or services (outside commercial policy)
  • Direct messages or private communication with students

Schools should not tag photos of children or name them in comments without specific permission. Even if a student is not identifiable, images that include a student must not be used without consent.

What employees cannot do

  • Connect with or communicate with students outside department platforms
  • Share any information about or including a student on personal social media
  • Use images, video, or audio that includes a student on personal social media, even if the student is not identifiable

The irony: an individual teacher posting a class photo to their personal Facebook would breach policy. But the school posting the same photo to its official public Facebook Page is standard practice.

Sources
  • Social media procedures, NSW Department of Education
  • Official School Social Media Rules of Engagement, NSW DoE

The consent process

The full analysis of the consent form is on the No Consent page. Here's how the process works in practice.

How it typically happens

  1. Enrolment: When a child starts at a NSW public school, parents are given the Department's standard "General Permission to Publish and Disclose Information" form.
  2. The choice: The form offers a single binary option. Consent to all public publishing (website, newsletter, social media, newspapers) or none of it.
  3. Duration: The signed permission "remains effective until I advise the school otherwise." There is no expiry date. No annual renewal is required (though it is recommended).
  4. Photos taken: Throughout the year, teachers and staff photograph school events, activities, excursions, sports days, assemblies, and classroom work.
  5. Photos posted: Staff with access to the school's Facebook Page post these photos, checking them against the consent register.
  6. Photos become public: The moment a photo is posted on the public Page, it is visible to every person on the internet, indexed by search engines, and accessible to automated scrapers.

The warning parents receive

The consent form includes this notice:

"Parents should be aware that when information is published on public websites and social media channels, it can be discoverable online for a number of years, if not permanently. Search engines may also cache or retain copies of published information. Published information can also be linked to by third parties." NSW Department of Education, General Permission to Publish form

This warning talks about discoverability and caching. It says nothing about AI training, facial recognition scraping, or inclusion in training datasets. That's not because the Department was being deceptive. It's because these risks did not exist in their current form when the policy was written.

What "specific consent" looks like

The NSW DoE privacy guidelines distinguish between general permission (the form above) and specific permission required for particular projects or posts that focus on one or a few students, or that relate to topical or public debate issues.

Specific consent requires a detailed privacy collection notice explaining:

  • The purpose of the images
  • Who the intended recipients are
  • How the images will be stored
  • Who to contact for access

In practice, most school Facebook posts fall under the general permission form. Specific consent is reserved for media appearances, promotional materials, and special projects.

The photography process

Who takes the photos

In most NSW public schools, particularly smaller ones, photos are taken by:

  • Teachers using school or personal devices during activities and events
  • Office/admin staff at assemblies, award ceremonies, and special events
  • The principal at school events and activities
  • Professional photographers for class photos (these are typically distributed privately, not posted on social media)

The NSW DoE has a specific rule about personal devices: "If a personal device is used the photographs should be uploaded to a school device and deleted from the personal device as soon as reasonably practicable. That deletion must include any cloud backups."

Who posts the photos

The account administrators post content to the school's Facebook Page. At minimum, this includes the principal or a school executive and at least one other staff member. In practice, it varies. Some schools have a dedicated "communications" person. Many do not, and posting falls to whoever has the login.

Content is moderated during school hours only: 9am to 3pm weekdays, excluding public holidays. Posts made outside these hours or comments from the public outside these hours go unmoderated until the next school day.

What happens to photos of parents who didn't consent

The NSW DoE privacy guidelines state that when consent is absent, "all information regarding the identity of the student needs to be removed" through cropping, blurring, blocking, or pixelation. In practice, at a busy school event with group photos, this is difficult to enforce perfectly.

The gap

Every element of this system was designed in good faith. The laws, the policies, the consent forms, the processes. They were built for a world where posting a photo on Facebook meant sharing it with a community. Nobody anticipated that "sharing" would mean:

  • Permanent extraction into AI training systems that cannot forget
  • Harvesting into facial recognition databases used by law enforcement and private companies
  • Inclusion in datasets used to build tools that can generate explicit images of children
  • Indefinite indexing by search engines and web archives

The system hasn't failed. It just hasn't caught up with what "public" means in 2026.

The policy itself requires updating

The NSW DoE requirement that school social media accounts "must not restrict access or be set as 'private'" made sense when the main risk was poor community engagement. It does not make sense when the consequence of public access is AI training, facial recognition, and deepfake source material. Advocating for a policy update is part of the longer-term work here. In the meantime, schools and P&Cs can start the conversation about what should change.

Summary of the legal landscape

Law / Policy What it covers What it misses
Privacy Act 1988 (Federal) Photos are personal information. Children need special care. Consent required for sensitive info. Does not directly cover NSW government schools. No mention of AI training or scraping.
PPIP Act 1998 (NSW) Covers NSW public schools. Sets information protection principles for collection, use, disclosure. No specific guidance on social media posting. No mention of AI or automated data collection.
Online Safety Act 2021 Platform accountability. Image-based abuse. Best interests of the child in platform design. Focused on platform obligations, not school obligations. Does not address school-posted content.
Under-16 ban (Dec 2025) Platforms must prevent under-16s from having accounts. Does not address adults posting children's images. Schools are not covered.
NSW DoE Social Media Policy Consent required for student images. Principal oversight. Moderation during school hours. Requires accounts to be public. No AI/scraping disclosure. Binary consent form. No annual renewal required.
Children's Privacy Code (due Dec 2026) Will set new standards for children's personal information online. Broader than social media. Not yet in effect. Scope and enforcement details still being finalised.
