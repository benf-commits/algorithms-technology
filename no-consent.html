Evidence

None of us consented to this

We all signed the form. We trusted the process. But the form doesn't mention AI, and the world it was written for no longer exists.

The form we all signed

If you're a parent at a NSW public school, you've signed one of these. It's the standard Department of Education "General Permission to Publish and Disclose Information" form, the legal basis for schools posting our children's photos on Facebook, the school website, newsletters, and in media. We signed it because we trust our schools. And because it seemed straightforward.

The form offers parents a single binary choice:

"I give permission to the school/Department of Education to publish information about my child as described above, including in publicly accessible communications."

OR

"I do not give permission to the school/Department of Education to publish information about my child as described above, including in publicly accessible communications."

That's it. One box or the other. The form bundles everything into a single consent:

  • Public websites including the school website
  • The Department of Education intranet (staff only)
  • School newsletters, magazines, and reports
  • Official school social media accounts on Facebook, YouTube, and Twitter
  • Local and metropolitan newspapers and other media outlets

The problem isn't that we signed it. It's what it doesn't say.

1. No granularity

The form doesn't let us say "yes to the school newsletter and website, no to public Facebook." It's all or nothing. If we're comfortable with our child appearing in a printed newsletter sent home in a school bag, we also have to accept their photo being posted on a public Facebook Page visible to every person on the internet, or opt out of everything.

Opting out of everything means our child doesn't appear in the school newsletter, isn't shown in the school magazine, isn't included in event photos. For a primary school child, that feels like being left out. The binary choice forces us to either accept public social media exposure or isolate our child from school community life. Most of us, understandably, tick the consent box.

2. No informed consent about AI

The form does include this warning:

"Parents should be aware that when information is published on public websites and social media channels, it can be discoverable online for a number of years, if not permanently. Search engines may also cache or retain copies of published information. Published information can also be linked to by third parties." NSW Department of Education, General Permission to Publish form

This warning was written for a pre-AI world. It talks about discoverability and caching. It says nothing about:

  • AI training: Meta scraping every public post to train its AI models
  • Facial recognition: Clearview AI and others harvesting faces into biometric databases
  • Deepfake generation: AI tools creating explicit or manipulated images from a single clear photo
  • Training dataset inclusion: photos ending up in datasets like LAION-5B that are used by AI developers worldwide
  • Permanence in AI systems: once a photo is used for AI training, it cannot be removed from the model

The consent parents gave was for "publishing." What's actually happening is "extraction into permanent AI systems." These are fundamentally different things, and no parent was asked to consent to the latter.

3. We signed in a different era

The Permission to Publish form remains effective "until I advise the school otherwise." Many of us signed this form years ago, when Facebook was primarily a way to share photos with friends and family. How could we have known what was coming?

  • 2007–2023: Meta was collecting data, but AI training hadn't been publicly disclosed
  • September 2024: Meta admitted to the Australian Senate it had been scraping all public posts for AI training
  • 2024: Human Rights Watch found Australian children in AI training datasets
  • 2024: Deepfake attacks on Australian schoolgirls made national news
  • December 2025: Australia banned children under 16 from social media platforms

A consent form signed in 2020, or 2018, or 2015, can't meaningfully apply to a risk landscape that didn't exist when it was signed. The consent is stale. The risks are new. The form needs to catch up.

4. Our children have no voice

The consent is between the school and us as parents. Our children, whose faces, names, and identities are being published, have no say. A 2024 peer-reviewed study found this violates Article 12 of the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child (the right to be heard in matters affecting them).

For our older primary school children, years 4, 5, and 6, this matters. They're old enough to understand what social media is and old enough to have opinions about whether their photos are posted publicly. The current framework doesn't ask them.

We banned our kids from social media, but kept posting their photos on it

In December 2025, Australia legislated to keep children under 16 off social media platforms. As a country, we decided these platforms aren't safe for our kids. 4.7 million under-16 accounts were removed.

But our schools, doing what they've always done with the best of intentions, are still posting our children's photos on those same platforms. Nobody thought to update the process. The law protects our children from being on social media. Nothing yet protects them from being published on it.

The good news: we can close this gap together

Australia is developing the Children's Online Privacy Code (due December 2026) and broader privacy law reform is underway. The system is catching up. But we don't have to wait for legislation to protect our own children. By working with our schools to update how they use social media, we can close this gap now, as a community, not as a mandate.

What informed consent would actually look like

A consent framework fit for 2026 would, at minimum:

  • Separate consent for different channels. School newsletter, school website, social media, and external media should each be separate opt-in decisions.
  • Disclose AI and data risks. Parents should be explicitly informed that public social media posts are scraped for AI training, facial recognition, and dataset collection.
  • Default to private. Social media posting should require specific, informed opt-in, not be bundled into a general consent.
  • Include the child's voice. Older children should be consulted about whether their images are shared publicly.
  • Regular re-consent. Given how rapidly the risk landscape changes, consent should be renewed annually, not left open-ended.

None of this exists in the current NSW framework. Reforming the Department of Education's consent process is a longer-term goal. The immediate step schools can take is to remove the most dangerous vector: the public Facebook Page.
