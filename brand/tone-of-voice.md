# algorithms.technology — Tone of Voice

## Who we are

We're parents. Some of us saw this coming. Some of us didn't. It doesn't matter. What matters is that now we all know, and we can do something about it.

## Voice principles

### 1. Straight

Say what's happening. Don't soften it. Don't hedge. If Meta admitted under oath that they scrape our children's photos for AI training, say that. The facts are alarming enough without exaggeration, and clear enough without spin.

- Don't dress up bad news
- Don't use weasel words ("may", "could potentially", "there is a risk that")
- If something is confirmed, say "confirmed." If it's happening right now, say "happening right now."
- Trust people to handle the truth

**Yes:** "Meta scrapes every public Facebook post to train AI. This includes photos of our children."
**No:** "There are growing concerns that social media companies may be utilising user-generated content in ways that could potentially impact children's digital privacy."

### 2. Clear

This is complicated technology. Our job is to make it understandable, not to show how much we know. Every sentence should make the reader more informed, not more confused.

- Short sentences over long ones
- Common words over technical ones
- When a technical term is needed, explain it immediately
- One idea per paragraph
- Structure with clear headings so people can find what they need

**Yes:** "Once a photo is used to train an AI model, it can't be removed. There's no delete button."
**No:** "The irreversible nature of machine learning model training means that data points incorporated into neural network weight matrices during the training process cannot be subsequently extracted or expunged."

### 3. Honest

Don't pretend things are simpler than they are. Don't promise a fix when there isn't one. Don't say "just do this one thing and your children are safe." Be upfront about what works, what doesn't, and what we don't know yet.

- If a private Group doesn't stop Meta's own AI training, say so
- If we can't undo the damage already done, say so
- If we're not sure about something, say so
- Never overstate the evidence. The real evidence is strong enough.

**Yes:** "Moving to a private Group stops third-party scraping. It doesn't stop Meta. Getting off Facebook stops Meta. We're starting with the first step."
**No:** "One simple change can protect your children from AI."

### 4. No judgement

People didn't know. Or they did know and felt powerless. Or they knew and figured it was fine. None of that matters now. We're not here to make anyone feel bad about what they've posted, what they've signed, or what they didn't understand. We're here to share what's happening and help people act on it.

- Never blame parents for posting photos
- Never blame schools for using Facebook
- Never imply people should have known better
- Never use "you should have" or "if only people had"
- The fault lies with the companies that built these systems, not the people who used them in good faith

**Yes:** "The consent form doesn't mention AI training. It was written before any of this existed."
**No:** "Parents who signed the consent form without reading it carefully exposed their children."

### 5. Empathetic

This is scary. People reading this might feel guilty, angry, overwhelmed, or helpless. Acknowledge that. Give them something concrete they can do. Don't leave them sitting in fear.

- Every problem section should lead to an action section
- Acknowledge the emotional weight of this information
- Don't be clinical when talking about children's safety
- Remember that behind every statistic is someone's kid

**Yes:** "This is hard to read. It was hard to write. But knowing is the first step to doing something about it."
**No:** "The data clearly demonstrates a statistically significant increase in risk vectors."

### 6. Hopeful and forward-looking

The situation is bad. But it's not hopeless. We can act. We can change things. Regulation is coming. Communities are waking up. Every school that moves to a private Group is one less source of publicly accessible children's photos. Focus on what we can do, not just what's been done to us.

- End sections with action, not despair
- Frame the ask as achievable
- Celebrate progress when it happens
- Position this as a movement, not a complaint

**Yes:** "Every school that makes this change protects its children. And every school that does it makes it easier for the next one."
**No:** "Unless urgent action is taken, the situation will only deteriorate further."

### 7. Direct

Get to the point. Don't warm up. Don't throat-clear. Don't write an introduction to the introduction. The first sentence of every section should tell you what that section is about.

- Lead with the finding, not the methodology
- Lead with the action, not the context
- If a paragraph doesn't add information, cut it
- If a word doesn't add meaning, cut it

## What we never do

- **Sensationalise.** The facts are alarming enough. We don't need clickbait.
- **Condescend.** We explain things clearly because this stuff is genuinely complex, not because we think people are stupid.
- **Moralise.** We present evidence and recommend action. We don't lecture.
- **Pretend.** Don't pretend we didn't know. Don't pretend it's simple. Don't pretend one change fixes everything. Be real.
- **Use em dashes.** Seriously. Use full stops, commas, colons, or restructure the sentence.

## Formatting rules

- **No em dashes.** Ever. Restructure the sentence.
- **Australian English.** Colour, organised, defence, programme (but program for software).
- **Short paragraphs.** 2-3 sentences max in body copy.
- **Active voice.** "Meta scrapes photos" not "Photos are scraped by Meta."
- **Present tense for current threats.** "This is happening" not "This has been happening."
- **Specific over general.** "362 Australian children" not "hundreds of children." "30 billion photos" not "billions of photos."
- **Name names.** Meta, Clearview AI, LAION. Not "social media companies" or "AI firms."
- **Source everything.** Every factual claim links to its source. No exceptions.

## The emotional arc

The site should take people on this journey:

1. **Awareness.** "I didn't know this was happening."
2. **Understanding.** "Now I get why it matters."
3. **Concern.** "This affects my kids."
4. **Agency.** "There's something I can do about it."
5. **Action.** "Here's exactly how."
6. **Community.** "Other parents and schools are doing this too."

Every page should move people forward on this arc, not leave them stuck at concern.
