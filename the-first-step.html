Evidence

This can't be fixed. But we can start.

Moving to a private Group is the first step, not the last. Here's what each level of action actually protects against, and what it doesn't.

Let's be honest with each other

We'd love to say there's a simple fix. There isn't. Our children's photos that have already been posted on public Facebook Pages have already been scraped. AI models trained on those photos can't forget them. Facial recognition databases that captured those faces will hold them indefinitely.

What's done is done. But we can stop it getting worse, together. And each step we take reduces our children's exposure going forward.

Three levels of protection

Level 1: Move to a private Facebook Group

The minimum first step. What we're asking schools to do right now.

Threat Protected?
Third-party scrapers (Clearview AI, LAION, etc.) Yes. Content not publicly accessible.
Search engine indexing Yes. Private Group content not indexed.
Random public access to children's photos Yes. Membership approval required.
Meta's own AI training No. Meta owns the platform and accesses all content.
Members screenshotting or sharing photos No. Any member can still save and share.
Historical scraping (already happened) No. Already in databases and models.
Why this step still matters

A private Group immediately eliminates the broadest vector of harm: unrestricted public access. It stops the indiscriminate, automated, mass-scale scraping by third parties. It doesn't solve everything, but it solves the most egregious problem: anyone, anywhere, with no connection to the school, can access and download every photo of every child. It's the difference between leaving the front door open and closing it.

Level 2: Get off Facebook entirely

The next step. Removes Meta from the equation.

Threat Protected?
Third-party scrapers Yes
Search engine indexing Yes
Random public access Yes
Meta's own AI training Yes. No content on Meta's platform.
Other platform AI training Depends on the alternative platform's policies
Historical scraping No. Already in databases and models.

Moving to a purpose-built school communication platform, such as the school's own app, a parent portal, or a platform designed for school communities, removes Meta entirely from the equation. No data on Meta's platform means no Meta AI training.

Alternatives to Facebook for school communication include:

  • School website with password-protected parent section. The school already has a website.
  • Dedicated school apps (Compass, Sentral, ClassDojo, Seesaw). Many schools already use these.
  • Email newsletters. Direct to parents, not publicly indexed.
  • The school's own parent portal. Controlled by the school, not a social media corporation.

The right alternative depends on the school community's needs. The key criterion is: does the school control the platform, or does a company that mines data for AI?

Level 3: Stop publishing identifiable children's photos online

The most protective approach. Not where we start, but where the conversation leads.

Threat Protected?
All scraping and AI training Yes. No digital images to scrape.
Facial recognition capture Yes
Deepfake source material Yes
Historical scraping No. Already in databases and models.

This is the most radical position and the most protective. It means schools share photos of activities and events without identifiable faces, or share photos only through channels that don't involve digital transmission (printed newsletters, physical displays in the school).

We're not advocating for this as an immediate step. Parents value seeing their children's school life. But it's worth acknowledging that every digital photo of a child, on any platform, carries some risk in the current landscape. The question is how much risk a school community is comfortable accepting.

What about the photos already out there?

This is the hardest part. Photos already posted on public Facebook Pages may have already been:

  • Scraped into Meta's AI training systems
  • Harvested by Clearview AI and other facial recognition databases
  • Included in training datasets like LAION-5B
  • Cached by search engines and web archives

Deleting these posts won't remove the data from systems that have already processed it. But deleting them does:

  • Prevent future scraping by new systems
  • Remove the photos from search engine results (after caches expire)
  • Reduce the surface area of publicly available images of each child
  • Send a clear signal that the school takes children's digital safety seriously

Cleaning up historical public posts is a necessary companion to moving forward with a private Group or alternative platform.

The direction of Australian regulation

Now
No specific protections for children's images on school social media
The NSW Permission to Publish form bundles everything together. No granularity. No AI disclosure.
December 2025
Under-16 social media ban in effect
Children can't be ON social media, but schools can still post their photos ON social media.
December 2026
Children's Online Privacy Code due
Will set new standards for how children's personal information is collected and used online.
Ongoing
Privacy Act reform
Comprehensive reform of the Privacy Act is underway, likely to strengthen children's protections significantly.

The direction is clear: stronger protections for children's data, less tolerance for unconsented use of personal information, and greater accountability for platforms that scrape and train on that data. Schools that act now are getting ahead of where the law is going. Schools that wait are accumulating risk.

What we're asking for, right now

What we're asking, together

We're not asking our schools to stop communicating with us. We're not asking them to abandon social media overnight. We love seeing our kids' photos from school events. We're asking for one specific, practical, free change: move from a public Facebook Page to a private Facebook Group.

This is Level 1. Closing the front door. It won't solve everything, and we owe each other honesty about that. But it immediately stops the worst of it: the indiscriminate public exposure of our children's photos to every scraper, every AI system, and every stranger on the internet.

Then we keep going. Together.
