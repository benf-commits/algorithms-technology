What We Can Do Together

Everything you need to start the conversation at your school. Free. Practical. No technical expertise required. We've done this ourselves, and here's how.

Why a private Group instead of a public Page?

Facebook Pages are designed to be public. They cannot be made private. Facebook Groups can be set to private, giving you full control over who sees your content.

Feature Public Page Private Group
Content visible to anyone on internet Yes No
Content indexed by search engines Yes No
Available for AI training scraping Yes No
Accessible to facial recognition scrapers Yes No
Membership approval required No. Anyone can follow. Yes. Admin approves.
Supports posts, photos, events, files Yes Yes
Two-way community conversation Limited Strong. All members notified.
Cost to switch Free
This is the first step, not the solution

A private Group stops third-party scrapers (Clearview AI, LAION, etc.) and removes children's photos from public search results. But Meta still has access to everything posted on its platform, including private Groups. The only way to fully stop Meta from using children's photos is to get off Facebook entirely. A private Group is the minimum viable first step, and it's still worth doing right now. Read the honest breakdown →

Community engagement actually improves

Research shows private Facebook Groups generate more engagement and interaction than public Pages. All members are notified when anyone posts (not just page admins), creating stronger community conversations. Groups also support features like files, polls, and year-level sub-groups that Pages don't offer.

How we can make this happen

This isn't a technology project. It's a conversation with our school community, followed by a simple settings change. Here's the path we're following.

Raise the issue at your P&C meeting

Use the motion template below. Present the key evidence: Meta's Senate admission, the Human Rights Watch findings, and the deepfake incidents. The goal is a formal P&C recommendation to the school principal.

Remember: The P&C cannot direct the school. But a formal, well-evidenced recommendation from the parent body carries significant weight with principals and the Department of Education.

Principal reviews and approves

The principal manages the school's official social media presence. Share the evidence package (link them to this site). Most principals will recognise the risk immediately. This is a duty-of-care issue, not a technology debate.

Create the new private Group

Create a new Facebook Group with privacy set to Private. Name it clearly (e.g. "[Your School Name] Community"). Set up admins from both the school and P&C. Add screening questions for membership requests (e.g. "What is your child's name and class?").

Migrate the community

Post on the existing public Page directing all followers to join the new private Group. Send the link via the school newsletter, email, and app. Pin a final post on the old Page with the link. Allow 2–4 weeks for migration.

Archive the public Page

Once the community has moved, remove all historical posts containing student images from the public Page. You can archive or unpublish the Page entirely, or leave it as a minimal redirect to the private Group. Do not just stop posting. The historical photos remain publicly accessible until removed.

Update consent processes

Review and update the school's Permission to Publish forms and processes. Consider adding granular consent options that distinguish between internal school communications and social media posting. This is optional but recommended as a best practice.

P&C Motion Template

Copy and adapt this motion for your P&C meeting. It includes the problem statement, evidence summary, proposed solution, and formal motion wording.

Item: School Facebook Page - Transition to Private Group

Background

[School name] currently operates a public Facebook Page for school communications and community engagement. Content posted on public Facebook Pages is visible to anyone on the internet without requiring a Facebook account or any connection to the school.

The issue

Since September 2024, it has been publicly confirmed that:

  1. Meta (Facebook's parent company) scrapes all public Facebook posts, including photos of children on adult/organisational accounts, to train its AI models. This was confirmed under oath to the Australian Senate by Meta's global privacy director. Australian users have no opt-out.
  2. Facial recognition companies including Clearview AI have scraped over 30 billion photos from Facebook, including children's images, to build biometric databases. The Australian Information Commissioner found this breached the Privacy Act.
  3. Human Rights Watch found 362 identifiable Australian children in the LAION-5B AI training dataset from a sample of less than 0.0001% of the data. Sources included school uploads.
  4. In June 2024, AI-generated explicit images of 50+ schoolgirls at a Melbourne school were created using their publicly accessible social media photos.

The NSW Department of Education's "Permission to Publish" consent form does not inform parents that their children's photos may be scraped for AI training, facial recognition databases, or used to generate deepfakes. The consent form offers only a binary choice (all public publishing or none) with no option to consent to newsletters but not public social media.

Proposed solution

Transition the school's Facebook presence from a public Page to a private Group. A private Group:

  • Restricts content visibility to approved members only
  • Removes children's photos from public access, search engine indexing, and AI scraping
  • Maintains all communication functionality (posts, photos, events, discussions)
  • Improves community engagement (research shows private Groups generate more interaction)
  • Costs nothing and can be implemented immediately

That the P&C strongly recommends the school transition its Facebook presence from a public Page to a private Group, and that all historical posts containing student images on the current public Page be removed, in order to protect students from AI data scraping, facial recognition harvesting, and other emerging privacy threats not contemplated by current consent forms.

Questions we've been asked

Won't we lose followers/reach?

You'll lose vanity metrics (follower count) but gain something more valuable: an engaged, verified community of actual parents and carers. Research consistently shows private Groups generate more meaningful interaction than public Pages. The people who matter, your school community, will join the Group.

Can't we just stop posting photos of children?

That addresses future risk but doesn't protect the children whose photos are already on the public Page. It also eliminates a valuable part of school communications. Parents love seeing what their children are doing at school. A private Group lets you keep sharing photos safely.

Does the P&C have the authority to make this change?

The P&C cannot direct the school on operational matters. However, a formal, well-evidenced recommendation from the P&C carries significant weight. The school's social media presence directly affects students and families, which is squarely within the P&C's remit of interest. The motion is worded as a strong recommendation, not a directive.

What about prospective families who want to see what the school is like?

The school website remains public and can showcase school life. A minimal public Facebook Page can remain as a "shopfront" with general information and a link to join the private Group. No child's identifiable photo needs to be on the public Page for prospective families to learn about the school.

Is making it private actually enough to stop scraping?

No system is perfect. However, a private Group removes content from public web access, search engine indexing, and the large-scale automated scraping that Meta, Clearview AI, and dataset builders rely on. It's the single most impactful step a school can take, and it's free.

What about other platforms (Instagram, TikTok, etc.)?

The same principles apply. Any platform where the school posts identifiable student images to a public account carries the same risks. Facebook is typically the primary platform for school communications, which is why we start here. The same evidence supports moving any school social media to private/restricted settings.

Will the Department of Education have a problem with this?

The NSW DoE's own social media guidelines require written consent before posting student images and warn that public content is permanently discoverable. Moving to a private Group is more aligned with the spirit of these guidelines, not less. No DoE policy requires a school to have a public Facebook Page.

Share this with other parents

This affects every school in Australia with a public Facebook Page, not just ours. If this helped you understand what's happening, share it with other parents and P&Cs. The more of us who know, the faster we can protect our children.
