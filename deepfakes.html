Evidence

Deepfakes are already targeting Australian children

AI-generated explicit images of schoolgirls. Created from their social media photos. This is not a future risk. It happened.

Bacchus Marsh Grammar, Melbourne, June 2024

In June 2024, at least 50 female students at Bacchus Marsh Grammar, a co-educational school on the outskirts of Melbourne, discovered that AI-generated nude images featuring their faces had been created and circulated online.

  • The victims were girls in years 9 to 12, aged approximately 14 to 18
  • The images were created using photos posted to social media. Publicly accessible photos of the girls' faces were fed into AI tools that generated explicit nude images.
  • The deepfakes were distributed on Instagram and Snapchat
  • A teenage boy was arrested and questioned by police
  • Victoria is the only Australian state where sharing deepfake pornography is a criminal offence, carrying a sentence of up to 7 years imprisonment
"Approximately 50 girls targeted in AI explicit photo scandal at Melbourne school." SBS News, June 2024

The parents of the victims described the experience as devastating. The girls had done nothing wrong. They had simply existed on social media with publicly visible photos. AI tools did the rest.

Gladstone Park Secondary College, Melbourne, 2025

In a separate incident, students at Gladstone Park Secondary College had their formal school photos edited using AI and shared by Year 11 students online and in group chats. Two students were suspended.

These weren't photos the victims had posted themselves. They were official school photos, the kind that schools routinely share on their public Facebook Pages.

The pipeline: from school photo to deepfake

The path from a school Facebook post to a deepfake is disturbingly short:

Four steps

1. School posts identifiable photos of children on a public Facebook Page
2. Those photos are accessible to anyone on the internet. No login, no connection to the school.
3. Freely available AI tools can generate explicit or manipulated images from a single clear face photo
4. The generated images are distributed. The child has no recourse. The original photos cannot be "unlearned."

The tools required for step 3 are widely available, often free, and require no technical expertise. A clear, well-lit photo of a child's face, exactly the kind posted on school Facebook Pages, is the ideal input for these systems.

The scale of the problem

The Bacchus Marsh and Gladstone Park incidents are the ones that made the news. The eSafety Commissioner has reported that the number of deepfake image reports is doubling year on year, with girls and women overwhelmingly targeted.

The Australian government's online safety agency has also reported that tens of millions of harmless images of minors originally shared on social media have been downloaded and saved on child exploitation sites. The images don't need to be manipulated to cause harm, but AI tools have made manipulation trivially easy.

Why school Facebook Pages are particularly dangerous

School Facebook Pages are an unusually rich source of children's images for bad actors because:

  • The photos are high quality. Schools post well-lit, clear photos, ideal for AI face-matching.
  • Children are identified. Posts often include first names, class groups, and the school name, making the child easily locatable.
  • The photos are public. No login, no friend request, no membership. Just a URL anyone can access.
  • There are many photos per child. Schools post throughout the year: sports days, excursions, classroom activities, assemblies. This builds a comprehensive set of facial images per child.
  • The school name is on the Page. Combined with a child's name and face, this is enough to identify and locate a specific child at a specific school.

Researchers at the University of Utah and Carnegie Mellon University described school Facebook pages as potentially "the largest existing collection of publicly accessible, identifiable images of minors" after analysing 18 million school Facebook posts.

Legal protections are thin

Victoria is the only Australian state where sharing deepfake pornography is specifically criminalised (up to 7 years imprisonment). In other states, victims may have to rely on general harassment, child exploitation, or image-based abuse laws that were not designed for AI-generated content.

At the federal level, the eSafety Commissioner can issue removal notices for image-based abuse material, but this is reactive. It addresses distribution after the fact, not creation or prevention.

Prevention is the only reliable protection. Once a photo is public, it can be captured in seconds. Once a deepfake is created, it can be distributed endlessly. The only intervention point that a school controls is whether or not it makes children's photos publicly accessible.

This is about what we can control

We can't control what AI tools exist or who uses them. But we can control whether our children's photos are sitting on a public Facebook Page, freely accessible to anyone. A private Group, or better still not posting on Facebook at all, closes the easiest door. That's something we can do together, right now.
